
## **Chapter 4.1 вАУ Basics of Counting Techniques**

### вЬЕ **Rule of Product (Multiplication Rule)**

When youвАЩre making a series of choices, and each choice is **independent**, you multiply the number of options.

> рЯФЄ If you have **a** ways to do one thing, and **b** ways to do another, then there are `a √Ч b` total ways to do both.

**Example:**
YouвАЩre ordering pizza.

* 2 crust choices: *thin*, *deep-dish*
* 3 toppings: *cheese*, *pepperoni*, *sausage*

Total combinations = `2 √Ч 3 = 6`

So you can have:

* thin+cheese
* thin+pepperoni
* thin+sausage
* deep+cheese
* deep+pepperoni
* deep+sausage

рЯОѓ **When to use it?** вЖТ When **both actions happen together**.

---

### вЮХ **Rule of Sum (Addition Rule)**

When you have **mutually exclusive** choices (you can do either one OR the other, not both), you **add** the number of options.

> рЯФЄ If there are **a** ways to do something and **b** ways to do something else, and you can do only one вАФ then there are `a + b` ways total.

**Example:**
Pizza shop gives **one free add-on**:

* 1 thick crust
* or 3 toppings

You pick **only one** of these вЖТ Total options = `1 + 3 = 4`

---

### рЯФД **Dependent Events and Factorials**

When choices depend on previous ones, use **factorials**.

> рЯФЄ `n!` (read as вАЬn factorialвАЭ) is the total number of ways to arrange **n** distinct objects.

**Example:**
John has 5 different books to arrange on a shelf:

вЖТ `5! = 5 √Ч 4 √Ч 3 √Ч 2 √Ч 1 = 120` ways

Each book placed affects whatвАЩs left to choose next вАФ so order matters.

---

### рЯПЖ **Example with Rankings:**

In a dog competition with 10 dogs, how many ways to choose **1st and 2nd place**?

* First place: 10 choices
* Second place: 9 choices (since 1 dog already taken)

вЖТ Total = `10 √Ч 9 = 90` ways

рЯУМ **Tip:** When you hear *вАЬfor eachвА¶вАЭ* вАФ thatвАЩs a sign to **multiply**.

---

### рЯФҐ **Permutations**

A **permutation** is about **arranging objects** where **order matters**, and **no repetition** is allowed.

---

#### рЯФЄ All objects:

If youвАЩre arranging **all n** items:

> `nPn = n!`

**Example:** Permutations of вАЬABCвАЭ

вЖТ ABC, ACB, BAC, BCA, CAB, CBA
вЖТ `3! = 6` total

---

#### рЯФЄ Some of the objects:

Arranging **r** items out of **n**, in order:

> `nPr = n! / (n - r)!`

**Example:** 2-letter permutations of вАЬABCвАЭ

вЖТ AB, AC, BA, BC, CA, CB
вЖТ `3P2 = 3! / (3 - 2)! = 6`

---

### рЯФШ **Combinations**

In a **combination**, **order does NOT matter**, and again, no repetition.

> рЯФЄ `nCr = n! / [r! √Ч (n - r)!]`

YouвАЩre just selecting **groups**, not arranging.

**Example:** 2-letter combinations from "ABC"

вЖТ AB = BA, AC = CA, BC = CB
So unique combos = AB, AC, BC вЖТ 3 in total

> `3C2 = 3`

---

### рЯФЇ **PascalвАЩs Triangle**

This triangle is a cool way to quickly find combination values (like C(n, r)). It also gives coefficients in binomial expansions like (a + b)вБњ.

```
Row 0:           1
Row 1:         1   1
Row 2:       1   2   1
Row 3:     1   3   3   1
Row 4:   1   4   6   4   1
Row 5: 1   5  10  10   5   1
```

Each number is the **sum of the two numbers above it**.
For example, in row 4, position 2 (value 6) is:

> `C(4,2) = 4! / (2! √Ч 2!) = 6`

---

**рЯУМ Final Tip:**

* Use **product** rule when choices are made **together**
* Use **sum** rule when choices are **either-or**
* Use **factorials** for arranging items
* Use **nPr** when order **matters**
* Use **nCr** when order **doesnвАЩt**

## **Chapter 4.2 вАУ Pigeonhole Principle & Inclusion-Exclusion Principle**

---

### рЯХ≥пЄП **Pigeonhole Principle (Simple Form)**

If you try to place **more items than containers**, at least **one container will hold more than one item**.

рЯУМ **Put simply**:
If **n+1** pigeons are put into **n** pigeonholes, **at least one** pigeonhole will have **2 or more** pigeons.

---

### вЬПпЄП Example 1:

**Q: WhatвАЩs the minimum number of students needed to ensure that at least 3 are born in the same month?**

* There are 12 months вЖТ 12 pigeonholes
* We want **3 students in the same month** вЖТ So **k+1 = 3 вЗТ k = 2**

According to the **generalized pigeonhole principle**:
To ensure **at least one month** has 3 birthdays, we need:
`n = 12 pigeonholes` and `students вЙ• 2√Ч12 + 1 = 25`

вЬЕ **Answer: 25 students**

---

### вЬПпЄП Example 2:

**Q: If 13 people are in a room, show that at least two share a birth month.**

There are only 12 months.

вЖТ 13 people into 12 months вЖТ By the pigeonhole principle,
**at least 1 month must have 2 or more people** born in it.

вЬЕ **Conclusion: Always true with 13 people.**

## рЯІЃ **Inclusion-Exclusion Principle**

This principle helps us **count how many elements are in a union of sets**, especially when some items **overlap** in multiple sets.

---

### вЬПпЄП Example:

**Q: How many numbers from 1 to 1000 are NOT divisible by 3, 5, or 7?**

LetвАЩs break it into sets:

* A = multiples of 3
* B = multiples of 5
* C = multiples of 7

We use:

* |A| = вМК1000/3вМЛ = **333**
* |B| = вМК1000/5вМЛ = **200**
* |C| = вМК1000/7вМЛ = **142**

Now their overlaps:

* |AвИ©B| = вМК1000/15вМЛ = **66**
* |BвИ©C| = вМК1000/35вМЛ = **28**
* |AвИ©C| = вМК1000/21вМЛ = **47**
* |AвИ©BвИ©C| = вМК1000/105вМЛ = **9**

Now total divisible by 3, 5, or 7 =
\= |A| + |B| + |C| вИТ |AвИ©B| вИТ |BвИ©C| вИТ |AвИ©C| + |AвИ©BвИ©C|
\= 333 + 200 + 142 вИТ 66 вИТ 28 вИТ 47 + 9 = **543**

Total not divisible = 1000 вИТ 543 = **457**

вЬЕ **Answer: 457 numbers**

## рЯІ© **How Pigeonhole Principle Connects to Functions**

LetвАЩs say you have a function f: A вЖТ B, where:

* A = pigeons (inputs)
* B = pigeonholes (targets)

We get 3 cases:

* **Case A (|A| > |B|):**
  Too many pigeons вЖТ at least one pigeonhole must hold multiple.
  вЗТ f is **surjective but not injective**
  рЯУМ *Pigeonhole Principle lives here!*

* **Case B (|A| = |B|):**
  Perfect mapping вЖТ each pigeon in its own hole.
  вЗТ f is **bijective**

* **Case C (|A| < |B|):**
  Some holes are empty.
  вЗТ f is **injective but not surjective**

---

## рЯФБ **Pigeonhole Proof (By Contradiction)**

Say you have **n pigeons** and **m pigeonholes** with **n > m**.

Suppose **no pigeonhole has more than one pigeon**.

Then:
Each pigeonhole has вЙ§ 1 pigeon вЖТ max pigeons = m √Ч 1 = m

But we said n > m вЖТ вЭМ Contradiction.

вЬЕ So, **at least one pigeonhole has 2 or more pigeons**.

---

### рЯФН Real-World Example: Hair Count

> *вАЬAt least two people in California have the same number of hairs.вАЭ*

Why?

* LetвАЩs say:

  * People in CA = over **40 million**
  * Max number of hairs on a head вЙИ **1 million**

Then we have:

* 40 million people (pigeons)
* Only 1 million possible hair counts (pigeonholes)

By pigeonhole principle вЖТ some people must share the same number of hairs!

вЬЕ **Always true**

---

## рЯО≤ Subset Sum Example (Advanced Pigeonhole)

> If you take any 10 numbers from 1 to 100, there will be **two subsets with the same sum.**

Why?

* Let A = set of 10 integers
* Total possible subsets = 2¬євБ∞ = **1024**
* Each subset has a sum from 0 to 1000 (at most) вЖТ only **1001** possible sums

1024 subsets trying to fit in 1001 possible sum values вЖТ
вЮ°пЄП **At least 2 subsets must have the same sum**

вЬЕ Pigeonhole strikes again!

## рЯІ† Chinese Remainder Theorem (Quick Idea)

If you know:

* x вЙ° a (mod m)
* x вЙ° b (mod n)

... and m & n are co-prime, you can always find **x**.

Why is pigeonhole involved?

* You create a sequence like `b, b + m, b + 2m, вА¶`
* Eventually, one of these will be вЙ° a mod n
* Because youвАЩre cycling through all mod n residues вЖТ pigeonhole principle guarantees a match!

### 4.3 Generalised Permutations and Combinations вАФ Easy Notes

LetвАЩs clear one thing up right away:
If **order matters**, itвАЩs a **permutation**.
If **order doesnвАЩt matter**, itвАЩs a **combination**.

---

### рЯФБ Permutations вАУ When Order Matters

Permutations are all about arranging items in **different orders**. Think of arranging people in a line, creating passwords, or figuring out race positions вАФ here, order is crucial.

**Basic formula for permutations (without repetition):**
**P(n, r) = n! / (n - r)!**
Where:

* n = total items
* r = items to arrange
* ! = factorial (e.g., 4! = 4√Ч3√Ч2√Ч1 = 24)

**Example:**
How many 2-letter arrangements can you make from A, B, C?
Answer: P(3,2) = 3! / (3-2)! = 6
(AB, AC, BA, BC, CA, CB)

---

### вЩїпЄП Permutations with Repetition

Sometimes, items can repeat (like digits in a password). Then itвАЩs simple:

**Formula:**
**n^r** (n to the power r)

**Example:**
3-letter words using letters from вАЬSMOKEвАЭ (5 letters) with repetition:
\= 5^3 = **125**

---

### рЯІЃ Permutations of Multisets

When some items **repeat**, like in the word вАЬBALLOONвАЭ (two Ls, two Os), we divide by the factorial of those repeats:

**Formula:**
**n! / (p1! √Ч p2! √Ч ... √Ч pk!)**

**Example:**
вАЬBALLOONвАЭ has 7 letters (n = 7), with:

* 2 Ls,
* 2 Os

So:
\= 7! / (2! √Ч 2!) = 5040 / (2√Ч2) = **1260**

---

### вЬЕ Combinations вАУ When Order DoesnвАЩt Matter

This is about selecting items **without caring about the order**. Think of choosing team members, lottery numbers, or flavors of ice cream.

**Formula for combinations:**
**C(n, r) = n! / (r! √Ч (n - r)!)**

**Example:**
Choosing 2 players from S, T, U:
C(3,2) = 3! / (2! √Ч 1!) = 3

So: ST = TS (only counted once)

---

### Real-Life Examples

* A 4-digit pin with **no repeated digits**:
  P(10,4) = 5040

* If **repetition is allowed**:
  10^4 = 10,000 combinations

---

### рЯТ° Special Cases

**Q: How many ways to arrange 6 kids in a line**
(i) If 2 specific kids are always together:
Treat them as one вЖТ now 5 items вЖТ 5!
The pair can be arranged in 2 ways вЖТ
**Total = 5! √Ч 2! = 120 √Ч 2 = 240**

(ii) If those 2 kids are never together:
Total arrangements = 6! = 720
Minus the вАЬtogetherвАЭ case = 720 - 240 = **480**

---

This is the foundation of counting problems in math and computer science. Just remember вАФ always ask: *Does order matter?* That tells you whether to use permutation or combination.

### рЯУШ 4.4 Recurrence Relations вАУ A Friendly Explanation

A **recurrence relation** is basically a way of defining a sequence by expressing each term using previous terms in the sequence. ItвАЩs like a recipe where each step depends on what you just did. These are commonly used in computer science and mathematics to describe patterns, sequences, and algorithm runtimes.

#### рЯФБ What Is a Recurrence Relation?

A recurrence relation (also called a **difference equation**) gives us a way to compute the next term of a sequence based on one or more previous terms.

**Example:**
The equation

```
f(x + 3h) + 3f(x + 2h) + 6f(x + h) + 9f(x) = 0  
```

is a recurrence relation. If we replace `f(x + h)` with something like `ar+1`, then it looks like:

```
ar+3 + 3ar+2 + 6ar+1 + 9ar = 0  
```

---

#### рЯУР Order and Degree of Recurrence Relations

* **Order**: The difference between the largest and smallest subscripts. It tells us how many past values are involved.
  рЯСЙ *Example:*
  `13ar + 20ar-1 = 0` вЖТ Order is **1** (only `ar` and `ar-1` involved)

* **Degree**: The highest power of the terms involved (like exponents).
  рЯСЙ *Example:*
  `yвВЦвВКвВГ¬≥ + 2yвВЦвВКвВВ¬≤ + 2yвВЦвВКвВБ = 0` вЖТ Degree is **3** (due to `yвВЦвВКвВГ¬≥`)

---

#### рЯІЃ Examples of Recurrence Relations

1. **Fibonacci Sequence**
   This is the classic example:

   ```
   FвВА = 0, FвВБ = 1  
   FвВЩ = FвВЩвВЛвВБ + FвВЩвВЛвВВ  
   ```

   Each term is the sum of the two before it.

2. **Factorial Sequence**
   Defined recursively as:

   ```
   n! = n √Ч (n-1)!  
   0! = 1  
   ```

---

#### вЬПпЄП General Formula

A recurrence relation is typically written like this:

```
xвВЩ = f(n, xвВЩвВЛвВБ, xвВЩвВЛвВВ, ..., xвВЩвВЛвВЦ)
```

Where:

* `xвВЩ` is the current term
* `k` is the order
* `f(...)` is the rule for computing it from previous terms

---

#### вЬЕ Solving Recurrence Relations

To solve them:

1. **Identify initial conditions** (e.g. `aвВА = 4`)
2. Use the recurrence rule to build the sequence step-by-step
3. Look for a pattern
4. Use the pattern to derive a **closed-form formula**

---

#### рЯІ† Sample Problem

**Problem:** Solve

```
aвВЩ = aвВЩвВЛвВБ вАУ n, where aвВА = 4
```

**Step-by-step Expansion:**

* aвВБ = 4 вАУ 1 = 3
* aвВВ = 3 вАУ 2 = 1
* aвВГ = 1 вАУ 3 = -2
* and so on...

LetвАЩs look at the pattern of differences:

```

aвВВ вАУ aвВБ = -2  
aвВГ вАУ aвВВ = -3

```

This forms the sum:

```

        = вАУn(n + 1)/2  
```

Now plug in `aвВА = 4`:

```

```

And thatвАЩs the closed-form expression!
